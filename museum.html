Shanghai Museum Mobile Application

Children physical fitness training

Type

Self-initiated redesign

My role

UX&UI designer

Deliverables

Duration

6 weeks

What I do?

Problem Analysis

As a large country-owned museum, Shanghai Museum has explored the digitization of its collections and virtual exhibition to a certain extent. And its services are extensive and comprehensive, which shows its academic support and years of business accumulation.

However, there are several problem with the existing official websites and applications.
(1) The key features and exhibition resources are not well connected through different screens and devices. Users have to use all of them to receive the wanted service at the same time.
(2)The current interface of exhibits information, 3D models and correspondingly virtual exhibition is immature and hard to use. Also, they are not accessible on one certain mobile application.

Objective

I have reorganized the featuress and services of the Shanghai Museum which should satisfy what users expect from a mobile application. It should be able to provide a smooth one-stop experience, such as browsing information about exhibitions of interest - booking - guided tours in the museum.
The mobile app will also maximize the use of digital resources from the precious collection, providing both a preview before visiting and an online virtual exhibition feature for those who cannot get to the site.

User Persona

There are two types of target users, in-person/local visitors and remote visitors. They can share many resources on the one hand. On the other hand, they will differ in the second half of the experiential behavior process. In-person viewers are less dependent on the virtual exhibition interface, online audience, and vice versa.
However, it is worth noting that the two types of users are not fixed, but will switch between each other. Even those who can reach the site can make decisions by means of an online virtual preview. Therefore, the user profiles of Kristy and Micheal are summarized with these two categories as references.

Kristy's User Map

Take Kristy as an example, as a potential in-person visitor, her objective of action is to book a suitable date and complete the exhibition visit.

Painpoint

Therefore, we can outline three user painpoints from it and design to solve them. Users want more info when booking a visit, more convenient virtual exhibition, more accesaible in-person visit

User flow

Wireframe

Based the hand-drawn draft, I designed and connected low-fidelity wireframes.There are two key focus and user flow:
(1) Browsing exhibition information and finding dates for appointment. I emphasize the information validity and completeness on the appointment page.
(2) Using the in-museum map to navigate and provide exhibit information. I strengthened the user-friendly,especially senior friendly operation, and the information sequence and attractiveness of the interface.

Font

Insight

In fact, I started at designing this product in Chinese, and then I redesigned the English version. I gained some insights from this process.
(1) Serif vs. sans serif. As the design system demonstrates, there is a similar distinction between serif and sans serif in Chinese typography as there is in English. At first, considering that a traditional museum might need to present a classic, elegant image, I chose to use a serif font. As the design progressed, however, I realized where large paragraphs of text needed to be presented, such as for the exhibit detail information, serif fonts did not perform well. In order o improve readability, I introduced a more minimalist sans-serif font and adjusted the visual proportions of them.
(2) Language translation. Translating the Chinese on the app interface to English reminded me once again of the fundamental goal of the project: to spread traditional culture and make it accessible to a diverse population. Not only translation, but I also adjusted the font and content hierarchy for better cross-cultural communication.

User Interface

High-fidelity Prototype

Exhibition & exhibit information

In order to attract more young people to traditional museums, the homepage and exhibition information are designed to be more attractive and brighter. For example, the homepage has a daily recommendation that introduces a story about an exhibit related to the current exhibition.

The design enhances the search and filtering functions, making it easier for users to learn about the exhibition content and even to browse the exhibits in advance. It can increase their interest in visiting in person.

Make an appointment

The Shanghai Museum has a stringent appointment policy, which needs the applicant's ID and other information. So I add a step-by-step tip at the top bar, and divide the entire reservation process into two steps.

1. First of all, the calendar highlights the opening date of the museum. At the same time, with the click action of picking the date in the calendar, it will show the exhibitions available on that day to assist users in selecting a best date.

2. Reservation confirmation page, clearly shows QR code and the option to cancel the appointment. It can be reopened in the personal profile.

Virtual exhibition

The museum map and indoor floor plan serve both in-person and remote visitors.

For in-person visitors.
1. Through a LAN connection, the exact location of the visitor can be tracked and displayed on the floor plan, thus it can automatically play an audio explanation of the exhibit in front of them.
2. Exhibit information page provides voice to text option, as well as more inclusive adjustments in the settings page, including volume level, text font size, etc.

For online visitors.
1. the map enhances the simulation of interactive experience, shaping the atmosphere of visiting the Shanghai Museum.
2. 360° visit tour is provided on the recommended browsing route with the ability to click on the exhibits within the screen.

More accessible digital resource

Compared to physical items, digital resources of exhibits should be more easily accessible to a wider range of people, rather than blindly applying technologies such as AR, increasing operational costs.

In the case of the high-definition ancient embroidery paintings, I added collection and download options, and used magnifying glasses and audio explanation features to assist people with disabilities.

Conclusion & Reflection

1. Solid user research is crucial. Quantitative analysis can reveal users' tendencies through statistics, while specific interviews can reveal users' pain points in more detail, thus building a vivid user portrait. In this regard, we hope to do more improvement and iteration in the future.
2. How far can the museum's virtual tour be developed in the future? Can it be combined with Bluetooth, NFC, and AR/VR technologies to achieve better results? With the growth of user experience design, it may be possible to get a more realistic and inclusive interactive experience through iteration.
3. What will be the further connection between the physical exhibition space and the virtual digital world? Will the physical exhibition space also change its composition as the user's viewing perspective and demand shifts? I think this is a question that museums and art practitioners will continue to explore together in the future.
